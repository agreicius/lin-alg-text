Matrix representations of transformations.

Given: \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) a linear transformation, \(\dim V=n\text{,}\) \(\dim W=m\text{.}\) First choose bases \(B=\{\boldv_1,\dots,\boldv_n\}\) for \(V\) and \(B'=\{\boldw_1,\dots,\boldw_m\}\) for \(W\text{.}\) These two bases give rise to two coordinate vector isomorphisms:

\begin{align*} _B\colon V\amp \rightarrow \R^n\\ [\hspace{5pt}]_{B'}\colon W\amp \rightarrow \R^m \end{align*}

Putting all three of these maps together yields the diagram

where the double-headed arrows indicate the fact that the coordinate maps are isomorphisms: i.e., we also have inverse maps \(([\hspace{5pt}]_B)^{-1}\text{,}\) \(([\hspace{5pt}]_{B'})^{-1}\) going up.

The matrix \(A\) representing \(T\) with respect to the bases \(B\) and \(B'\) will be the matrix that “completes” this diagram by supplying an arrow from \(\R^n\) to \(\R^m\text{.}\)

Crucial in our discussion will be the theorem stating that

  1. a linear transformation can be defined simply by declaring where basis elements are sent, and

  2. the linear transformation is uniquely determined by this choice.
