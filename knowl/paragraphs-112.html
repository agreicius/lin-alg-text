Examples: \alert{$\R^n$}.

We define \(\R^n\) to be the set of all \(n\)-tuples: i.e., \(\R^n=\{(t_1,t_2,\dots t_n)\colon t_i\in\R \}\text{.}\) We call \(\R^n\) \(n\)-space, call elements of \(\R^n\) \(n\)-vectors, and denote elements of \(\R^n\) with bold, lowercase letters: typically, \(\boldv\text{,}\) \(\boldw\text{,}\) and \(\boldu\text{.}\)

Define a vector space structure on \(\R^n\) as follows.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any \(\boldv=(v_1,\dots,v_n)\) and \(\boldw=(w_1,\dots,w_n)\) in \(\R^n\) define:

\begin{align*} r\boldv\amp :=\amp (rv_1,rv_2,\dots ,rv_n) \text{ for all \(r\in\R\) } ,\\ \boldv+\boldw\amp :=\amp (v_1+w_1,\dots,v_n+w_n) \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp (0,0,\dots,0)\\ -\boldv\amp :=\amp (-v_1,-v_2,\dots,-v_n)\text{.} \end{align*}

The difference between \(\R^n\) and \(M_{1n}\) (row vectors) is simply one of notation (parenthesis instead of bracket). In particular, it follows immediately from the last example that \(\R^n\) with these operations is a vector space.

Furthermore I reserve the right to identify \(\R^n\) with \(M_{1n}\text{,}\) as well as \(M_{n1}\) (column vectors), in any given example. I will always make my choice explicit: e.g. ``Let \(V=\R^n\text{,}\) with elements treated as column vectors".
