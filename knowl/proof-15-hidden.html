We will prove this for lower triangular matrices, the proof for upper triangular matrices being exactly similar.

We proceed by induction on the size \(n\) of the matrix.

Base case: n=1..

If \(A=[a]\) is \(1\times 1\text{,}\) then \(\det(A)=a\text{,}\) which is the product of its diagonal entries.

Induction step..

We assume that the proposition is true for all lower triangular matrices of size \(n-1\text{.}\)

Let \(A=[a_{ij}]_{n\times n}\text{.}\) We compute \(\det(A)\) by expanding along the top row. Since \(A\) is lower triangular all the entries are 0 except \(a_{11}\) and we get \(\det(A)=a_{11}M_{11}\text{.}\)

Now \(M_{11}=\det(\tilde{A})\text{,}\) where \(\tilde{A}\) is the submatrix of \(A\) obtained by deleting the first row and column. This matrix is also lower triangular with diagonal entries \(a_{22},a_{33},\dots, a_{nn}\text{.}\) By the induction hypothesis \(\det(\tilde{A})=a_{22}\cdot a_{33}\cdots a_{nn}\text{.}\)

Thus \(\det(A)=a_{11}\det(\tilde{A})=a_{11}\cdot a_{22}\cdots a_{nn}\text{,}\) as claimed.
