The second statement about swapping columns follows from the first and the fact that \(\det A=\det A^T\text{.}\) I leave the details to you.

To prove the first statement we proceed by induction on \(n\text{.}\)

Base case: \(n=2\text{.}\).

Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\c\amp d \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Then \(\tilde{A}=\abcdmatrix{c}{d}{a}{b}\text{.}\) We have \(\det(A)=ad-cb=-(cb-ad)=-\det(\tilde{A})\text{,}\) as claimed.

Induction step..

Assume \(n\geq 3\text{.}\) We assume by induction that the result holds for any square matrices of size \(n-1\text{.}\)

Suppose \(\tilde{A}\) is the result of swapping the \(i\)-th and \(j\)-th rows of \(A\text{.}\) Compute the determinants of \(A\) and \(\tilde{A}\) by expanding along any row other than the \(i\)-th or \(j\)-th rows. This is possible since \(n\geq 3\text{.}\) Call this the \(k\)-th row.

The expansions for \(\det(A)\) and \(\det(\tilde{A})\) differ only in terms of their minors, \(M_{k\ell}\) and \(\tilde{M}_{k\ell}\text{,}\) and these differ only in that the square matrices of size \(n-1\) involved have two rows swapped: the ones corresponding the \(i\)-th and \(j\)-th rows of \(A\text{!}\)

By induction the determinants of these matrices differ by a factor of \((-1)\text{.}\) Thus \(M_{k \ell}=-\tilde{M}_{k\ell}\) for all \((k,\ell)\text{,}\) from which it follows that \(\det(A)=-\det(\tilde{A})\text{.}\)
