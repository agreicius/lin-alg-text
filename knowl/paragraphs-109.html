Examples: \alert{$M_{mn}$}.

As mentioned above, our inspiration for the abstract notion of a vector space comes from matrices. Let's make explicit how they constitute an example. Fix an \(m\) and \(n\) and let \(V=M_{mn}\) be the set of all \(m\times n\) matrices.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any \(A=[a_{ij}]\text{,}\) \(B=[b_{ij}]\text{,}\) define:

\begin{align*} rA\amp :=\amp [ra_{ij}], \text{ (the usual scalar multiplication) }\\ A+B\amp :=\amp [a_{ij}+b_{ij}], \text{ (the usual matrix addition) } \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp 0_{mn}, \text{ the \(m\times n\) zero matrix }\\ -A\amp :=\amp [-a_{ij}]\text{.} \end{align*}

We have already seen in Chapter 1. Section 1.3 that these operations, along with our choices of zero vector and additive inverses, satisfy all the axioms of our vector space definition. Thus \(M_{mn}\text{,}\) along with these operations, constitutes a vector space.
