Section 2.3 Further properties

Chapter 2. Section 2.3: further properties of the determinant.

Our analysis of how the determinant changes with row operations leads to a number of very important theorems.

Theorem 2.3.1. Determinant is multiplicative.

Let \(A,B\) be \(n\times n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Comment:.

please observe, the same is NOT true of addition!

\begin{equation*} \det(A+B)\alert{\ne}\det(A)+\det(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

Your instructor will do this in class.

Theorem 2.3.2. Determinant and invertibility.

Let \(A\) be \(n\times n\text{.}\) Then \(A\) is invertible if and only if \(\det(A)\ne 0\text{.}\)

Proof.
(\(\Rightarrow\)).

I'll prove this direction as a chain of implications.

\begin{align*} A \text{ invertible } \amp \Rightarrow AA^{-1}=I_n \text{ for some matrix \(A^{-1}\)}\\ \amp \Rightarrow \det(AA^{-1})=\det I_n\\ \amp \Rightarrow \det(AA^{-1})=1 \amp \text{ (since \(\det I_n=1\)) }\\ \amp \Rightarrow \det A\ \det A^{-1}=1 \amp \text{ (by Th. \knowl{./knowl/th_detmult.html}{\text{Theorem 2.3.1}}) }\\ \amp \Rightarrow \det A\ne 0 \amp \text{ (otherwise product would be \(0\)) } \end{align*}
\((\Leftarrow)\).

I'll prove the contrapositive of this direction: that is, if \(A\) is not invertible, then \(\det A=0\text{.}\)

Suppose \(A\) is not invertible, and suppose \(A\) reduces to the reduced row echelon matrix \(U\text{.}\) By the invertibility theorem, \(U\) is not the identity matrix. Thus one of its diagonal entries must be 0, and hence \(\det U=0\text{.}\)

This means we have \(E_rE_{r-1}\cdots E_1A=U\text{,}\) for some elementary matrices \(E_i\text{,}\) in which case \(\det E_r\det E_{r-1} \cdots \det E_1\det A=\det U=0\text{,}\) since \(\det\) is multiplicative. Since the \(E_i\) are invertible, \(\det E_i\ne 0\) (as shown above). It follows that \(\det A=0\text{.}\)

Adjoint formula for $A^{-1}$.

Its worth recording also that we can use the determinant to give an actual formula for computing the inverse of a matrix!

We all love formulas, but keep in mind that it is faster to use our Gaussian elimination algorithm for computing \(A^{-1}\text{.}\)

Theorem 2.3.3. Adjoint formula.

Let \(A\) be \(n\times n\text{,}\) and suppose \(\det(A)\ne 0\text{.}\)

We define the adjoint matrix of \(A\) as

\begin{equation*} \adjoint(A)=\left([C_{ij}]\right)^T; \end{equation*}

that is, the \(ij\)-th entry of the adjoint matrix is the \(ji\)-th cofactor of \(A\text{.}\)

Then

\begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det(A)}\adjoint(A)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

Let \(B=\adjoint A\text{,}\) and let \(c=\det A\text{.}\) Using the definition of \(\adjoint A\) and properties of the determinant, you can in fact show directly that

\begin{equation*} AB=c I_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

It follows that \(A^{-1}=\frac{1}{c}B=\frac{1}{\det A}\adjoint A\text{.}\)

Our growing invertibility theorem.

Thanks to Theorem 2.3.2, our invertibility theorem has grown by one statement.

Theorem 2.3.4. Invertibility theorem.

Let \(A\) be \(n\times n\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.

  1. \(A\) is invertible.

  2. \(A\boldx=\boldzero\) has a unique solution (the trivial one).

  3. \(A\) is row equivalent to \(I_n\text{,}\) the \(n\times n\) identity matrix.

  4. \(A\) is a product of elementary matrices.

  5. \(A\boldx=\boldb\) has a solution for every \(n\times 1\) column vector \(\boldb\text{.}\)

  6. \(A\boldx=\boldb\) has a unique solution for every \(n\times 1\) column vector \(\boldb\text{.}\)

  7. \(\det(A)\ne 0\text{.}\)
