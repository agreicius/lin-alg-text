Example: orthogonal projection revisited.

Let \(T\colon \R^3\rightarrow\R^3\) be orthogonal projection onto the plane \(\mathcal{P}: x+y+z=0\text{,}\) as defined earlier. We would like to derive a formula for \(T\text{,}\) which amounts to finding the \(A\) such that \(T=T_A\text{.}\)

As previously observed we have \(A=[T]_B\text{,}\) where \(B\) is the standard basis for \(\R^3\text{.}\) We can compute \([T]_B\) by first computing \([T]_{B'}\) for a cleverly chosen nonstandard basis \(B'\text{,}\) and then using the change of basis formula.

As done previously, we let \(B'=\{(1,-1,0), (1,0,-1), (1,1,1)\text{.}\) Since \(T\) maps the first two vectors to themselves, and the third vector to \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) we have \([T]_{B'}=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) (Go back to original example for details.)

Then

\begin{align*} A\amp =[T]_B=\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}[T]_{B'}\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P}\\ \amp =\begin{bmatrix}[rrr] 1\amp 1\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0\amp 1\\ 0\amp -1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \left( \begin{bmatrix}[rrr] 1\amp 1\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0\amp 1\\ 0\amp -1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\right)^{-1} =\frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}[rrr] 2\amp -1\amp -1\\ -1\amp 2\amp -1\\ -1\amp -1\amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

Lo and behold, we have rediscovered our matrix formula for orthogonal projection onto \(\mathcal{P}\text{!!}\)

(Note: since \(B\) is the standard basis in this case, \(\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}\) was easy to compute. )
