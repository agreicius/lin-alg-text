Chapter 3. Section 3.3: subspaces.
Definition 3.3.1.

Let \(V\) be a vector space. A subset \(W\subseteq V\) is a subspace of \(V\) if

  1. \(\boldzero\in W\text{,}\)

  2. \(\boldw_1, \boldw_2\in W\Rightarrow \boldw_1+\boldw_2\in W\text{,}\) (i.e., \(W\) is closed under addition),

  3. \(\boldw\in W\Rightarrow k\boldw\in W\) for all \(k\in\R\) (i.e., \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication).

Comments.

(1) You may see slightly different definitions for subspace in the literature: e.g., some define a subspace to be a subset \(W\subseteq V\) which, under the operations of addition and scalar multiplication of \(V\text{,}\) is itself a vector space. I prefer mine as it gives you a hands-on method of deciding whether a given subset of \(W\subseteq V\) is in fact a sub{space} of \(V\text{:}\) namely, determine whether each of properties (i)-(iii) hold for \(W\text{.}\)

(2) Each of properties (ii) and (iii) is stated as an implication. Thus when verifying these, as always we assume the antecedent and prove the consequent.

Example.

Let \(V=\R^2\) and let \(W\subseteq V\) be the subset \(W=\{(t,t)\in\R^2 \colon t\in\R\}\text{.}\) We claim \(W\) is a subspace.

Proof.

We must show properties (i)-(iii) hold for \(W\text{.}\)

(i) The zero element of \(V\) is \(\boldzero=(0,0)\text{,}\) which is certainly of the form \((t,t)\text{.}\) Thus \(\boldzero\in W\text{.}\)

(ii) We must prove the implication \(\boldw_1, \boldw_2\in W\Rightarrow \boldw_1+\boldw_2\in W\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \boldw_1,\boldw_2\in W\amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldw_1=(t,t), \boldw_2=(s,s) \text{ for some \(t,s\in\R\) }\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldw_1+\boldw_2=(t+s,t+s)\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldw_1+\boldw_2\in W\text{.} \end{align*}

(iii) We must prove the implication \(\boldw\in W\Rightarrow k\boldw\in W\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \boldw\in W\amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldw=(t,t)\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp k\boldw=(kt,kt)\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp k\boldw\in W \end{align*}
Example.

Let \(V=\R^n\text{,}\) and treat elements of \(V\) as column vectors. Fix a vector \(\boldv_0\in V\text{.}\) Let \(W\subseteq V\) be the subset \(W=\{\boldw\in \R^n\colon \boldv_0^T\boldw=0\}\text{.}\) We claim \(W\) is a subspace.

Proof.

We must show properties (i)-(iii) hold for \(W\text{.}\)

(i) The zero element of \(V\) is \(\boldzero=\colvec{0\\ \vdots\\ 0}\text{.}\) Clearly \(\boldv_0^T\boldzero=0\text{.}\) Thus \(\boldzero\in W\text{.}\)

(ii) We must prove the implication \(\boldw_1, \boldw_2\in W\Rightarrow \boldw_1+\boldw_2\in W\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \boldw_1,\boldw_2\in W\amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldv_0^T\boldw_1=0,\boldv_0^T\boldw_2=0\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldv_0^T(\boldw_1+\boldw_2)=\boldv_0^T\boldw_1+\boldv_0^T\boldw_2=0+0=0\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldw_1+\boldw_2\in W\text{.} \end{align*}

(iii) We must prove the implication \(\boldw\in W\Rightarrow k\boldw\in W\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \boldw\in W\amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldv_0^T\boldw=0\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp \boldv_0^T(k\boldw)=k\boldv_0^T\boldw=k0=0\\ \amp \Rightarrow\amp k\boldw\in W \end{align*}
Lines and planes.

Recall that a line in \(\R^2\) \alert{containing the origin \((0,0)\)} can be expressed as the set of solutions \((x_1,x_2)\in\R^2\) to an equation of the form

\begin{equation*} ax_1+bx_2=0, \text{ or } \colvec{a\\b}^T\colvec{x_1\\ x_2}=0 \end{equation*}

Similarly, a plane in \(\R^3\) \alert{containing the origin \((0,0,0)\)} can be expressed as the the set of solutions \((x_1,x_2,x_3)\) to an equation of the form

\begin{equation*} ax_1+bx_2+cx_3=0, \text{ or } \colvec{a\\b\\c}^T\colvec{x_1\\x_2\\x_3}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the previous example we see that lines in \(\R^2\) containing the origin are subspaces, as are planes in \(\R^3\) containing the origin! Both can be expressed in the form \(W=\left\{\boldx\colon \boldv_0^T\boldx=0\right\}\text{:}\) take \(\boldv_0=(a,b)\) in the first case, and \(\boldv_0=(a,b,c)\) in the second.

On the other hand, a line or place that does not contain the origin is not a subspace, since it does not contain \(\boldzero\text{.}\)

We will revisit this example after defining the dot product on \(\R^n\text{.}\)

In fact these are all sub{spaces} of \(V\text{.}\) Proof: (i) the zero function, \(h(x)=0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) is \(\infty\)-differentiable, and thus is an element of each of these sets; (ii)-(iii) these sets being closed under addition and scalar mult. is a consequence of calculus results about sums and scalar multiples of continuous/differentiable functions.
Polynomials.

Recall that a polynomial is a function that can be written in the form \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{,}\) where \(a_i\in\R\) are fixed constants and \(a_n\ne 0\text{.}\) We call \(n\) the degree of such a function, denoted \(\deg f=n\text{.}\)

Let \(P\) be the set of all polynomial functions, and let \(P_n=\{f\colon f(x)=\anpoly, a_i\in\R \}\) be the set of all polynomials of degree at most \(n\text{.}\)

We have the set inclusions \(P_n\subset P\subset C^\infty(X)\text{,}\) where \(X\) is any nontrivial interval. (The second inclusion holds since any polynomial is infinitely differentiable on any interval. )

It is easy to see that in fact \(P_n\) is a subspace of \(P\text{,}\) and \(P\) is a subspace of \(C^\infty(X)\text{.}\) Indeed \(P_n\) and \(P\) both contain the zero polynomial \(f(x)=0=0x^n+0x^{n-1}+\cdots 0x+0\text{,}\) which is the same thing as the zero function \(0_X=\boldzero\text{.}\) Furthermore, sums and scalar multiples of polynomials (resp. of polynomials of at most degree \(n\)) are polynomials (resp. polynomials of at most degree \(n\)).

Important:.

a fact we will make use of all the time is that for two polynomials

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} f(x)=\anpoly, \ a_n\ne 0 \\ g(x)=\bmpoly, \ b_m\ne 0 \end{array} \end{equation*}

we have \(f(x)=g(x)\) if and only if (1) n=m, and (2) \(a_i=b_i\) for all \(0\leq i\leq n\text{.}\)

Intersections and unions.
Theorem 3.3.2.

Let \(V\) be a vector space, and suppose \(W_1,W_2,\dots, W_r\) are each subspaces of \(V\text{.}\) Then the intersection \(W=W_1\cap W_2\cdots \cap W_r\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)

Comment.

Thus intersections of subspaces are subspaces. The same is not true of unions.

For example, take \(V=\R^2\text{,}\) \(W_1=\{(t,t)\colon t\in\R\}\) and \(W_2=\{(t,-t)\colon t\in\R\}\text{.}\) Then each \(W_i\) is a subspace, but their union \(W_1\cup W_2\) is not. Why?

We have \(\boldw_1=(1,1)\in W_1\subset W_1\cup W_2\) and \(\boldw_2=(1,-1)\in W_2\subset W_1\cup W_2\text{,}\) but \(\boldw_1+\boldw_2=(2,0)\notin W_1\cup W_2\text{.}\)

Note that \(W_1\cup W_2\) is in fact closed under scalar multiplication.

Subsection 3.3.1 Span

Linear combinations and span.

Recall that a linear combination in a vector space \(V\) is a vector of the form

\begin{equation*} \boldv=k_1\boldv_1+k_2\boldv_2\cdots +k_r\boldv_r\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(k_i\in \R\) are scalars.

We use this notion to define the span of a set of vectors.

Definition 3.3.3.

Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(S=\{\boldv_1,\boldv_2,\dots,\boldv_r\}\) be a set of vectors of \(V\text{.}\) The span of \(S\) is the set

\begin{align*} \Span(\{\boldv_1,\boldv_2,\dots,\boldv_r\})\amp :=\amp \left(\begin{array}{c} \text{ the set of all linear } \\ \text{ combinations of the \(\boldv_i\) } \end{array} \right)\\ \amp =\amp \{\boldv\in V\colon \boldv=k_1\boldv_1+k_2\boldv_2\cdots +k_r\boldv_r, \text{ for some } k_i\in\R \}\text{.} \end{align*}
Theorem 3.3.4.

Let \(V\) be a vector space, \(S=\{\boldv_1,\boldv_2,\dots,\boldv_r\}\) a set of vectors of \(V\text{,}\) and \(W=\Span(S)\text{.}\) Then

  1. \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{;}\)

  2. if \(W'\) is a subspace containing all the vectors \(\boldv_i\text{,}\) then \(W\subseteq W'\text{.}\)

We paraphrase (ii) by saying \(W=\Span(S)\) is the “smallest” subspace of \(V\) containing all the \(\boldv_i\text{.}\)

More terminology:.

in the spirit of the last theorem, given a set of vectors \(S=\{\boldv_1,\dots, \boldv_r\}\text{,}\) we call \(W=\Span(S)\) the subspace of \(V\) generated by the vectors \(\boldv_i\text{.}\) Similarly, given a subspace \(W\text{,}\) a set \(S=\{\boldv_1,\dots, \boldv_r\}\) for which \(W=\Span(S)\) is called a spanning set of \(W\text{.}\)

Examples.

\alert{\(M_{mn}\)}. Define \(E_{ij}\) to be the matrix whose \((i,j)\)-th entry is 1, and whose every other entry is 0. Then the set

\begin{equation*} \{ E_{ij}\colon 1\leq i\leq m, 1\leq j\leq n\} \end{equation*}

is a spanning set for \(M_{mn}\text{.}\)

\(\R^n\).

In a similar vein, define \(\bolde_i\) to be the \(n\)-tuple whose \(i\)-th entry is 1, and whose every other entry is 0.

Then \(\{\bolde_1, \bolde_2, \dots, \bolde_n\}\) is a spanning set for \(\R^n\text{.}\)

\(P\) and \(P_n\).

The set \(\{1, x, x^2, \dots, \}\) is a spanning set for \(P\text{.}\) The set \(\{1, x, x^2, \dots, x^n\}\) is a spanning set for \(P_n\text{.}\)

\(\R^\infty\).

As above we can define \(\bolde_i\in \R^\infty\) to be the infinite sequence whose \(i\)-th entry is 1, and whose every other entry is \(0\text{.}\)

Note, however, that the set \(\{\bolde_1, \bolde_2, \bolde_3, \dots\}\) is not a spanning set for \(\R^\infty\text{.}\)

Indeed, the sequence \((1,1,1,\dots)\) is not a (finite!) linear combination of the \(\bolde_i\text{.}\)

\alert{\(\R_{>0}\)} Any \(a\ne 1\in \R_{>0}\) forms a spanning set for \(\R_{>0}\) as a vector space. This is because scalar multiplication by \(r\) in \(\R_{>0}\) is defined as exponentiation. Thus \(\Span(\{a\})=\{a^r\colon r\in\R\}=\R_{>0}\text{.}\) The last equality holds since the exponential function \(f(x)=a^x\) has range all positive reals for any base \(a\ne 1\text{.}\)

Example.

Let \(V=P_2\) and let \(S=\{p_1, p_2\}\text{,}\) where \(p_1(x)=x^2-1\) and \(p_2(x)=x^2-x\text{.}\) Show that \(W=\Span(S)\) is the subspace of all polynomials \(p(x)=a_2x^2+a_1x+a_0\) for which \(p(1)=0\text{.}\) That is:

\begin{equation*} \Span(S)=\{p(x)\in P_2\colon p(1)=0\} \end{equation*}
Proof.

We wish to prove a set equality. We do so by showing the \(\subseteq\) and \(\supseteq\) relations separately. (See my proof technique guide!)

\(\subseteq\).

Note first that \(p_1(1)=p_2(1)=0\text{.}\) Given an element \(q(x)\in \Span\left(\{p_1(x), p_2(x)\}\right)\text{,}\) we have \(q(x)=ap_1(x)+bp_2(x)\) for some \(a, b\in \R\text{.}\) But then \(q(1)=ap_1(1)+bp_2(1)=0+0=0\text{.}\) Thus \(q(x)\in \{p(x)\colon p(1)=0\}\text{.}\)

\(\supseteq\).

Now take \(p(x)=a_2x^2+a_1x+a_0\in \{p(x)\in P_2\colon p(1)=0\}\text{.}\) We must find \(a, b\in \R\) such that \(p(x)=ap_1+bp_2\text{.}\)

Since \(p(1)=0\text{,}\) we have \(a_2+a_1+a_0=0\text{.}\) I claim \(p(x)=(-a_0)p_1+(-a_1)p_2\text{.}\) Indeed we have

\begin{equation*} -a_0p_1-a_1p_2=(-a_0-a_1)x^2+a_1x+a_0=a_2x^2+a_1x+a_0\text{,} \end{equation*}

since \(a_2+a_1+a_0=0\text{.}\) This shows that \(p(x)\in\Span\left(\{p_1, p_2\}\right)\text{,}\) as desired.

Let \(V=P_2\) and let \(S=\{p_1, p_2, p_3\}\) where

Subsection 3.3.2 Null space

Null space of a linear transformation.

The notion of span is a useful tool for constructing subspaces inside a space \(V\text{:}\) given any collection \(\{\boldv_1, \boldv_2, \dots, \boldv_r\}\text{,}\) we now know that the set \(W=\Span(\{\boldv_1,\boldv_2,\dots, \boldv_r\})\) is guaranteed to be a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)

The notion of a null space of a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) provides another useful tool for constructing subspaces.

Definition 3.3.5.

The null space of a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) is the set

\begin{equation*} \NS T\colon =\{\boldv\colon T(\boldv)=\boldzero_W \}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the special case where \(T\colon \R^n\rightarrow \R^m\) and \(T=T_A\text{,}\) we may write \(\NS A\) for \(\NS T_A\text{:}\) by definition this is the set

\begin{equation*} \NS A=\NS T_A=\{\boldx\in \R^n\colon A\boldx=\underset{m\times 1}{\boldzero}\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In other words, \(\NS A\) is the set of solutions to the homogenous matrix equation \(A\boldx=\boldzero\text{,}\) or equivalently, its associated homogenous system of equations.

As we see in the next theorem, given linear \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{,}\) its null space \(\NS T\) is always a subspace !!

Theorem 3.3.6. Null space theorem.

Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{.}\) Then \(\NS T\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)

Proof.

  1. Since \(T(\boldzero_V)=\boldzero_W\text{,}\) we see that \(\boldzero_V\in \NS T\text{.}\)

  2. Suppose \(\boldv_1, \boldv_2\in \NS T\text{.}\) We have

    \begin{align*} T(\boldv_1+\boldv_2)\amp =T(\boldv_1)+T(\boldv_2) \amp \text{ (\(T\) is linear) }\\ \amp =\boldzero_W+\boldzero_W \amp \text{ (since \(\boldv_1,\boldv_2\in\NS T\)) }\\ \amp =\boldzero_W\text{.} \end{align*}

    Thus \(\boldv_1+\boldv_2\in \NS T\text{,}\) proving the implication \(\boldv_1,\boldv_2\in\NS T\Rightarrow \boldv_1+\boldv_2\in\NS T\text{.}\)

  3. Suppose \(\boldv\in\NS T\text{.}\) We have

    \begin{align*} T(c\boldv)\amp =cT(\boldv) \amp \text{ (\(T\) is linear) }\\ \amp =c\boldzero_W \amp \text{ (since \(\boldv\in\NS T\)) }\\ \amp =\boldzero_W\text{.} \end{align*}

    This shows \(c\boldv\in\NS T\text{,}\) proving the implication \(\boldv\in\NS T\Rightarrow c\boldv\in\NS T\text{.}\)

Since \(\NS T\) satisfies the conditions (i)-(iii), we see that it is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)

Examples.

The theorem gives us a clever, indirect way of proving a subset \(V'\subseteq V\) is a subspace: namely, find a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) for which \(V'=\NS T\) !!

Example 3.3.7.

The set \(W\subseteq\R^3\) of all vectors \((x,y,z)\) satisfying \(x+2y+3z=x-y-z=0\) is a subspace of \(\R^3\text{.}\)

Indeed we have \(W=\NS A\) where \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 2\amp 3\\ 1\amp -1\amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)

Example 3.3.8.

The set \(W=\{A\in M_{nn}\colon A^T=A\}\text{,}\) consisting of all symmetric \(n\times n\) matrices, is a subspace of \(M_{nn}\text{.}\)

Indeed, we have \(W=\NS T\text{,}\) where \(T\colon M_{nn}\rightarrow M_{nn}\) is the linear transformation defined as \(T(A)=A^T-A\text{.}\)

(I leave it to you to show \(T\) is linear.)

Example 3.3.9.

The set \(W\) of all infinitely differentiable functions \(f\) satisfying the differential equation \(f''(x)+xf'(x)=3f(x)\) is a subspace of \(C^\infty(\R)\text{.}\)

Indeed \(W\) is \(\NS T\text{,}\) where \(T\colon C^\infty(\R)\rightarrow C^\infty(\R)\) is the linear transformation defined as \(T(f)=f''+xf'-3f\text{.}\)

(I leave it to you to show \(T\) is linear.)

Subsection 3.3.3 Range of a linear transformation

Range.
Definition 3.3.10.

In general given a function \(f\colon A\rightarrow B\) with domain \(A\) and codomain \(B\text{,}\) its range is the set

\begin{equation*} \range f=\{b\in B\colon b=f(a) \text{ for some \(a\in A\) } \}=f(A)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the last equality makes use of our “image of a set” notation \(f(A)\text{.}\)

We will use the same notation for a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{.}\) The range of \(T\) is a subset of the codomain \(W\text{.}\) Not surprisingly, it is in fact a subspace of \(W\text{.}\)

Range.
Theorem 3.3.11. Range is a subspace.

Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) be a linear transformation. Then \(\range T\) is a subspace of \(W\text{.}\)

Proof.

  1. We must show \(\boldzero_W\in \range(T)\text{.}\) But \(\boldzero_W=T(\boldzero_V)\text{.}\) Thus \(\boldzero_W\in\range(T)\text{.}\)

  2. Suppose \(\boldw_1,\boldw_2\in \range(T)\text{.}\) This means there are \(\boldv_1, \boldv_2\in V\) such that \(T(\boldv_i)=\boldw_i\) for \(i=1,2\text{.}\) We must show \(\boldw=\boldw_1+\boldw_2\in\range(T)\text{.}\) Set \(\boldv=\boldv_1+\boldv_2\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{align*} T(\boldv)\amp =\amp T(\boldv_1+\boldv_2)\\ \amp =\amp T(\boldv_1)+T(\boldv_2) \ \text{ (since \(T\) is a linear transformation) }\\ \amp =\amp \boldw_1+\boldw_2=\boldw\text{.} \end{align*}

    Since we have provided a \(\boldv\) with \(T(\boldv)=\boldw_1+\boldw_2\text{,}\) we see that \(\boldw_1+\boldw_2\in\range(T)\text{.}\)

  3. Suppose \(\boldw\in\range(T)\text{.}\) Then there is a \(\boldv\in V\) with \(T(\boldv)=\boldw\text{.}\) Then \(T(k\boldv)=kT(\boldv)=k\boldw\text{,}\) showing \(k\boldw\in\range(T)\text{.}\)

Example.

Let \(T=T_A\colon \R^2\rightarrow\R^3\text{,}\) where \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\\ 2\amp 1\\ 3\amp 5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) According to the theorem, \(\range T_A\) is a subspace of \(\R^3\text{.}\) Can we identify this subspace as a familiar geometric object?

By definition \(\range T_A\) is the set

\begin{equation*} \{\boldy\in\R^3\colon \boldy=T_A(\boldx) \text{ for some \(\boldx\in \R^3\) } \}=\left\{\boldy=\begin{bmatrix}a\\ b\\ c \end{bmatrix} \colon \boldy=A\boldx \text{ for some \(\boldx\in\R^2\) } \right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus to compute \(\range T_A\) we must determine which choice of \(\boldy=(a,b,c)\) makes the system \(A\boldx=\boldy\) consistent. We answer this using our good, old friend Gaussian elimination!

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\amp a\\ 2\amp 1\amp b\\ 3\amp 5\amp c \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{ row reduction } }\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\amp a\\ 0\amp 1\amp 2a-b\\ 0\amp 0\amp -7a+2b+c \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

To be consistent we need \(-7a+2b+c=0\text{.}\) We conclude that \(\range T\) is the set of all \((a,b,c)\) satisfying \(-7a+2b+c=0\text{.}\) Geometrically this is the plane passing through \(O\) with normal vector \(\boldn=(-7,2,1)\text{.}\)

Example.

Consider again the linear transformation \(T\colon M_{nn}\rightarrow M_{nn}\text{,}\) \(T(A)=A^T-A\text{.}\) We saw that \(\NS T\) was the space of symmetric matrices. The theorem above tells us that \(\range T\) is also a subspace of \(M_{nn}\text{.}\) What is it?

Take \(B\in \range T\text{.}\) By definition this means \(B=T(A)=A^T-A\) for some \(A\text{.}\) So one, somewhat unsatisfying way of describing \(\range T\) is as the set of all matrices of the form \(A^T-A\text{.}\)

Let's investigate further. Notice that if \(B=A^T-A\text{,}\) then \(B^T=(A^T-A)^T=(A^T)^T-A^T=A-A^T=-B\text{.}\) Thus every element \(B\in \range T\) satisfies \(B^T=-B\text{.}\) Such matrices are called skew-symmetric.

I claim further that in fact \(\range T\) is the the set of all skew-symmetric matrices. To prove this, I need to show that given a skew-symmetric matrix \(B\text{,}\) there is a matrix \(A\) such that \(T(A)=B\text{.}\)

Suppose I have a \(B\) such that \(B^T=-B\text{.}\) Let \(A=-\frac{1}{2}B\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} T(A)=T(-\frac{1}{2}B)=-\frac{1}{2}(B^T-B)=-\frac{1}{2}(-B-B)=B\text{.} \end{equation*}

This shows that \(B\in \range T\text{,}\) and concludes the proof that \(\range T\) is the set of all skew-symmetric matrices.
