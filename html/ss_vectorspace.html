Section 3.1 Real vector spaces

Chapter 3. Section 3.1: executive summary.
Definitions:.

general vector space. \alert{Procedures:} deciding whether something is a vector space \alert{Theorems:} no theorems, but a list of familiar examples to get acquainted with.

Interlude on abstraction.

In our study of matrices, we have seen that operations from real number arithmetic (addition, subtraction, multiplication) have “analogues” in the world of matrices (matrix addition, matrix subtraction, matrix multiplication).

The question naturally arises as to what other types of mathematical objects (besides real numbers or matrices) admit analogous operations, and further, what we can say about these analogous systems.

A common technique in mathematics to help investigate such questions, is to distill the important properties of the motivating operations into a list of axioms, and then attempt to prove statements about any system of operations that satisfies these axioms.

We now embark on just such an endeavor. In the current setting we are concerned with two important operations defined on matrices: matrix addition (\(A+B\)) and scalar multiplication (\(cA\)). As you recall from Chapter 1. Section 1.4, these two operations satisfy many useful properties: e.g, commutativity, associativity, distributivity, etc.

We axiomatize this setting by considering any system admitting two operations like this: our axioms will stipulate the existence of these two operations, as well as the fact that the operations satisfy the relevant properties (commutativity, associativity, etc.). A system satisfying these axioms is called a vector space.

Chapter 3. Section 3.1: vector spaces.
Definition 3.1.1.

A (real) 1  vector spaceis a set \(V\) together with two operations defined on \(V\text{:}\) a multiplication by (real) scalars

\begin{align*} \R\times V\amp \rightarrow\amp V\\ (r,\boldv)\amp \mapsto\amp r\boldv\text{,} \end{align*}

called scalar multiplication, and an addition of elements of \(V\)

\begin{align*} V\times V\amp \rightarrow\amp V\\ (\boldv,\boldw)\amp \mapsto\amp \boldv+\boldw\text{,} \end{align*}

called vector addition.

There is a notion of complex vector space as well. In this course it is assumed the vector space is real. As such, we will often drop this modifier.

Furthermore, these two operations must satisfy the axioms listed on the next slide!

Definition contd..

  1. \(\boldv+\boldw=\boldw+\boldv\) (addition is commutative)

  2. \(\boldu+(\boldv+\boldw)=(\boldu+\boldv)+\boldw\) (addition is associative)

  3. There is an element \(\boldzero\in V\) satisfying \(\boldzero+\boldv=\boldv\) for all \(\boldv\in V\text{.}\) (\(V\) has an additive identity)

  4. For all \(\boldv\in V\text{,}\) there is an element \(-\boldv\in V\) s.t. \(\boldv+(-\boldv)=\boldzero\text{.}\) (\(V\) has additive inverses)

  5. \(r(\boldu+\boldv)=r\boldu+r\boldv\) (scal. mult. distributes over vector sums)

  6. \((r+s)\boldv=r\boldv+s\boldv\) (sum of scalars distributes)

  7. \(r(s\boldv)=(rs)\boldv\) (scal. mult. is assoc.)

  8. \(1\boldv=\boldv\) for all \(\boldv\in V\) (scalar 1 acts as identity)

Elements of a vector space \(V\) are called vectors.

A linear combination in a vector space is any expression of the form \(c_1\boldv_1+c_2\boldv_2+\cdots +c_r\boldv_r\text{,}\) where \(c_i\in\R\) and \(\boldv_i\in V\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\)

Examples: \alert{$M_{mn}$}.

As mentioned above, our inspiration for the abstract notion of a vector space comes from matrices. Let's make explicit how they constitute an example. Fix an \(m\) and \(n\) and let \(V=M_{mn}\) be the set of all \(m\times n\) matrices.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any \(A=[a_{ij}]\text{,}\) \(B=[b_{ij}]\text{,}\) define:

\begin{align*} rA\amp :=\amp [ra_{ij}], \text{ (the usual scalar multiplication) }\\ A+B\amp :=\amp [a_{ij}+b_{ij}], \text{ (the usual matrix addition) } \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp 0_{mn}, \text{ the \(m\times n\) zero matrix }\\ -A\amp :=\amp [-a_{ij}]\text{.} \end{align*}

We have already seen in Chapter 1. Section 1.4 that these operations, along with our choices of zero vector and additive inverses, satisfy all the axioms of our vector space definition. Thus \(M_{mn}\text{,}\) along with these operations, constitutes a vector space.

Examples: \alert{$\R^n$}.

We define \(\R^n\) to be the set of all \(n\)-tuples: i.e., \(\R^n=\{(t_1,t_2,\dots t_n)\colon t_i\in\R \}\text{.}\) We call \(\R^n\) \(n\)-space, call elements of \(\R^n\) \(n\)-vectors, and denote elements of \(\R^n\) with bold, lowercase letters: typically, \(\boldv\text{,}\) \(\boldw\text{,}\) and \(\boldu\text{.}\)

Define a vector space structure on \(\R^n\) as follows.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any \(\boldv=(v_1,\dots,v_n)\) and \(\boldw=(w_1,\dots,w_n)\) in \(\R^n\) define:

\begin{align*} r\boldv\amp :=\amp (rv_1,rv_2,\dots ,rv_n) \text{ for all \(r\in\R\) } ,\\ \boldv+\boldw\amp :=\amp (v_1+w_1,\dots,v_n+w_n) \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp (0,0,\dots,0)\\ -\boldv\amp :=\amp (-v_1,-v_2,\dots,-v_n)\text{.} \end{align*}

The difference between \(\R^n\) and \(M_{1n}\) (row vectors) is simply one of notation (parenthesis instead of bracket). In particular, it follows immediately from the last example that \(\R^n\) with these operations is a vector space.

Furthermore I reserve the right to identify \(\R^n\) with \(M_{1n}\text{,}\) as well as \(M_{n1}\) (column vectors), in any given example. I will always make my choice explicit: e.g. ``Let \(V=\R^n\text{,}\) with elements treated as column vectors".

Examples: \alert{the trivial vector space}.

Vector spaces of the form \(\R^n\) will be our main object of study. It is important to observe, however, that there are many other, more exotic vector spaces. In other words, you cannot always assume that a vector is an \(n\)-vector. We now introduce some of these more exotic examples, starting with the trivial vector space.

Let \(V=\{\boldv\}\) be a set containing exactly one element.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Define:

\begin{align*} r\boldv\amp :=\amp \boldv \text{ for all \(r\in\R\) } ,\\ \boldv+\boldv\amp :=\amp \boldv \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp \boldv\\ -\boldv\amp :=\amp \boldv\text{.} \end{align*}

It follows easily that \(V=\{\boldv\}\text{,}\) along with these operations and choices of zero vector and additive inverses, satisfies the definition of a vector space.

We call \(V\) the trivial (or zero) vector space, and denote it \(V=\{\boldzero\}\text{.}\) (Note that the single element of the trivial vector space is always the zero vector of the space. Hence the notation.)

Examples: \alert{$\R^\infty$}.
Definition 3.1.2.

We define \(\R^\infty\) to be the set of all infinite sequences of real numbers: i.e.,

\begin{equation*} \R^\infty=\{(t_1,t_2,t_3,\dots)\colon t_i\in\R \}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Define a vector space structure on \(\R^\infty\) as follows.

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any \(\boldv=(v_1,v_2,\dots)\) and \(\boldw=(w_1,w_2, \dots )\) in \(\R^n\) define:

\begin{align*} r\boldv\amp :=\amp (rv_1,rv_2,\dots) \text{ for all \(r\in\R\) } ,\\ \boldv+\boldw\amp :=\amp (v_1+w_1,v_2+w_2, \dots) \end{align*}
Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp (0,0,\dots)\\ -\boldv\amp :=\amp (-v_1,-v_2,\dots)\text{.} \end{align*}

It is easy to show \(\R^\infty\) with these operations forms a vector space.

Examples: \alert{$C^\infty(X)$}.

Let \(X\subset\R\) be any interval of any sort: e.g. \(X=[a,b]\text{,}\) \(X=(-\infty, \infty)\text{,}\) \(X=(-\infty, b]\text{,}\) etc. Define \(V=C^\infty(X)\) to be the set of all infinitely-differentiable real-valued functions with domain \(X\text{.}\)

Addition and scalar multiplication.

Given any elements \(f, g\in C^\infty(X)\text{,}\) define:

\begin{align*} rf\amp :=\amp \text{ the function defined as \((rf)(x)=rf(x)\) for all \(r\in\R\) } ,\\ f+g\amp :=\amp \text{ the function defined as \((f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\). } \end{align*}

In other words, vector scalar multiplication is defined to be scalar multiplication of functions, and vector addition is defined to be function addition.

Additive identity and inverses.

Set:

\begin{align*} \boldzero\amp :=\amp \text{ the zero function \(0_X\), defined as \(0_X(x)=0\) for all \(x\in X\). }\\ -f\amp :=\amp \text{ the function defined as \((-f)(x)=-f(x)\) for all \(x\in X\) } \text{.} \end{align*}

The fact that \(C^\infty(X)\) with these operations forms a vector space follows from familiar properties of function arithmetic.

Take a moment to let the exotic quality of this example sink in. Our “vectors” in this example are functions! Whereas we can concretely describe the general element of \(\R^3\) (\((a,b,c)\)) or even \(\R^n\) (\((r_1,r_2,\dots, r_n)\)), this is not so in \(C^\infty(X)\text{:}\) the general element is just some infinitely differentiable function \(f\text{.}\) I can give you plenty of examples of elements (e.g., \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) \(f(x)=\sin x-e^x\text{,}\) etc.), but I cannot express the general element in any kind of finite way.

It is now an easy, but interesting exercise to show that these definitions turn \(\R_{>0}\) into a vector space.

When proving a general fact about vector spaces we can only invoke the defining axioms the vector space satisfies; we cannot assume the vectors of the space are of any particular form. For example, we cannot assume vectors of \(V\) are \(n\)-tuples, or matrices, etc. We end with an example of such an axiomatic proof.

Theorem 3.1.3.

Let \(V\) be a vector space.

  1. \(0\boldv=\boldzero\) for all \(\boldv\in V\text{.}\)

  2. \(k\boldzero=\boldzero\) for all \(k\in \R\text{.}\)

  3. \((-1)\boldv=-\boldv\) for all \(\boldv\in V\text{.}\)

  4. If \(k\boldu=\boldzero\text{,}\) then \(k=0\) or \(\boldu=\boldzero\text{.}\)

Proof.

We prove (a), leaving (b)-(d) as an exercise.

First observe that \(0\boldv=(0+0)\boldv\text{,}\) since \(0=0+0\text{.}\)

By Axiom (e) we have \((0+0)\boldv=0\boldv+0\boldv\text{.}\) Thus \(0\boldv=0\boldv+0\boldv\text{.}\)

Add \(-0\boldv\text{,}\) the additive inverse of \(0\boldv\text{,}\) to both sides of the last equation:

\begin{equation*} -0\boldv+0\boldv=-0\boldv+0\boldv+0\boldv.\tag{\(*\)} \end{equation*}

The left-hand side of \((*)\) is \(\boldzero\text{,}\) by Axiom (d). The right-hand side is \(\boldzero+0\boldv=0\boldv\text{,}\) by Axiom (c). We conclude that \(\boldzero=0\boldv\text{.}\)
